Gravity and The Orbits of Celestial Bodies

Have you noticed how the moon revolves around the Earth, and how the Earth revolves around the Sun?

Start

Background

The path that the Moon takes as it revolves around the Earth, and the path the Earth takes as it revolves around the Sun, is called an orbit. This curved, regular path is a fundamental pattern in our solar system, with the Moon being Earth's natural satellite and the Earth orbiting the Sun due to the Sun's powerful gravitational force.

Purpose

The purpose of our article is to share information about gravity and how it controls the orbits of planets, moons, and other objects in space. We want to explain why planets closer to the Sun move faster, while planets farther away move slower. Our goal is to show that gravity and speed work together to keep orbits stable and prevent planets from crashing into the Sun or flying away. To make it clearer, we will also use a simulation to show how gravity affects motion. By combining facts and observations, we hope readers can better understand how gravity shapes the movement of celestial bodies.

Background Theories

An orbit is when celestial bodies (e.g. planets, satellites, asteroids, comets) move in a repeating circular pattern around a fixed point, usually a celestial body that’s bigger such as the sun. Orbits are caused and affected by gravity.

Experiment

Experiment Objective

To create a model that shows whether the heavier ball orbits faster or slow.

Variable used

Independent Variable: Distance of orbiting marbles

Dependent Variable: Time it takes for balls to revolve

Controlled Variable: The stretchy fabric

Hypothesis

If the orbiting ball is closer to the center mass, then it’ll fall and revolve around the center at a faster speed.

Materials and Tools

Hula hoop / embroidery hoop
Stretchy fabric (Lycra)
Heavy ball
Marbles
Tape or clips
Ruler & tape 
Stopwatch/phone
Dumbell

Procedures

  • 1. Stretch the fabric tightly over the hoop and secure it with tape/clips.
  • 2. Put the heavy ball in the center. It makes a dip like gravity.
  • 3. Use masking tape to mark circles at 5 cm, 10 cm, 15 cm, and 20cm from the middle.
  • 4. Gently roll a marble around the center.
  • If it’s too slow → it spirals in and crashes.
  • If it’s just right → it loops around a few times.
  • If it’s too fast/straight → it escapes.
  • 5. Pick one circle (for example, 5 cm). Start the timer when the marble passes a spot, count 3 laps, stop the timer.
  • 6. Divide the total time by 3 → that’s the orbit time.
  • 7. Do it 3 times at the same circle and find the average.
  • 8. Repeat the same for 5cm, 10cm, 15cm, and 20cm.
  • 9. Compare:
  • Which distance had the fastest orbit? Which had the slowest?
  • Was the orbit faster or slower with the heavier ball?

Conclusion

Based on our experiments, our hypothesis is proven correct because the balls that orbited around the center mass took more time to fall into orbit than those that were closer to the sun. As you can see from the data, the farthest ball (20cm) took an average of 9.073s to fall into orbit, while the closest ball (5cm) took an average of 4.447s to fall into orbit.
